"The Bar is in Hell". That's what legendary sales leader Leslie Venetz told us when I asked her about the current state of outbound sales. It still looks like 2011, with spray and pray SDR cadences and pipeline at all costs. The only thing 'predictable' about this way of going to market is that your prospects tune out, and you'll churn through the market.

The tactics that once built pipeline now erode trust, misalign teams, and burn markets. Sales development teams are still running motions from a decade ago – high-velocity cadences, automation-first outreach, and volume-based compensation models.

In this episode, I sat down with Leslie Venetz and CRO School co-founder Adem Manderovic to unpack not just what's broken, but what a modern outbound sales strategy should look like – one rooted in trust, relevance, and market validation.

## Outbound Sales Strategy is Still in 2011

Outbound today still looks suspiciously like it did in 2011.

> "Most teams are still running playbooks from 2011 – spray and pray SDR cadences and pipeline at all costs" — Leslie Venetz

And that approach hasn't aged well. Buyers have changed. Expectations have changed. But our outbound motions haven't.

The legacy of Predictable Revenue is part of the issue. It promised scalable pipeline with repeatable steps, but it left teams over-reliant on brute force. As Leslie put it, "We got told to do more of it very poorly instead of focusing on conversion ratio or if we're even targeting the right people". By chasing speed and volume, many teams lost sight of whether the outreach was even useful to the buyer.

A practical first step? Audit the last 50 outbound emails. Don't look at open rates or reply rates. Read them like a buyer. Would you respond? Would you trust the brand behind it?

## Principle #1 – "Earn the Right" Before Every Ask

Leslie has become known for one hell of a catch-phrase – "Earn the right". It stems from a key problem in outbound being not just poor execution, but entitlement. Too many messages go out assuming the buyer owes us their time.

She applies one filter to every touchpoint:

> "What have I written that earns me the right to even ask for their attention?" — Leslie Venetz

This idea of "earning the right" isn't a tagline, it's foundational. Leslie shared how her publisher initially pushed back on using "trust" in the subtitle of her book. She fought to keep it in because, in her words, "It's so fundamental to the way that I think about go-to-market".

A simple gut check: if your outreach is mostly about what you want (a call, a demo, a reply), and not what they need, you haven't earned it yet. Shift the focus. Lead with relevance. Prove your message belongs.

## Principle #2 – Trust Is the Ultimate KPI

Leslie put it plainly: "Buyers don't trust us. We don't trust execs. Execs don't trust reps. Reps don't trust marketers. It is eroded at every level".

Trust is the multiplier. Without it, even the best-targeted campaigns fall flat. As Leslie said later in the episode, "It works today and sabotages your ability to have durable long-term success". Short-term tactics might earn a few replies, but they often burn future conversations.

## Relevance Beats Hyper-Personalisation in Any Outbound Sales Strategy

One of the biggest missteps in outbound today is mistaking personalisation for relevance. Leslie summed it up: "If you have to choose one, relevance is more important".

She highlighted a common trap – spending an hour crawling through someone's LinkedIn or Instagram to mention something trivial, rather than investing that same time understanding their category-level challenges. That kind of surface-level personalisation often feels creepy, not helpful.

And relevance doesn't just mean "I see you're hiring" or "Congrats on your Series A." Leslie described the flood of outbound emails she receives that score in the 90s on email grading tools, yet "say nothing." Her words: "You've scored high, but what did you actually tell me?"

So build relevance at the persona level. Understand the triggers that matter to that type of buyer. Personalisation is the cherry on top. Relevance is the base of the cake.

## Fix SDR Metrics & GTM Alignment

Leslie nailed it: "We bonus SDRs on meetings booked, not pipeline influenced. Why would they care?" If the KPI is booked meetings, the incentive is to push for volume – regardless of whether those meetings are with the right people, at the right time.

This is what she calls the "SDR Industrial Complex" – a system optimised to push activity, not outcomes. It leads to teams doing "more wrong stuff, faster". Shift the measurement from activity-based metrics (meetings booked) to ones that drive real value (insights gathered, pipeline quality).

## Strategic Territory & Market Validation – Why You Need to Catalogue

Leslie made the point that the old-school mindset of being "CEO of your territory" has all but disappeared. "That's how I was trained… you knew your low-hanging fruit, you knew who your lookalikes were, you knew who you needed to call strategically".

That's where cataloguing comes in. Rather than blasting accounts based on arbitrary firmographics, teams should log first-person insights: which vendors they're using, when contracts are up, what frustrations they've voiced. Leslie pointed out that layering on intent signals can feel strategic, but without first-person insight, it's still guesswork. These tools show who's surging – not why they'd choose you. Cataloguing gives you the why.

## Smart Use of AI – Tools Assist, Humans Decide

Leslie cut straight to it: "You can't hack earning trust". The danger with tools like ChatGPT or Clay isn't the tech itself, it's the belief that the tech can replace understanding the buyer.

When used properly, AI can assist a smart strategy. Leslie acknowledged that tools like Omni have helped compress hours of research into minutes, yet she still insists on being the one to "connect the dots" herself. Use it to go deeper, not broader. Let it handle the admin. But never let it write the message for you.

## Putting It Into Action

Outbound has evolved – but most strategies haven't. Volume-first tactics, disconnected metrics, and automated messages can't build pipeline in markets where trust is low and attention is earned. Get back to cataloguing. "Earn the right" for their time. Track buyer-led signals, not just booked meetings. Outbound should be compounding, not cannibalising. Every message either builds trust or burns it.
